Meanwhile, image sensors available in scientific cameras are now larger, faster, and more sensitive compared to those previously available in science-grade cameras. This advances in digital imaging technology, combined with the latest generation of GECIs have greatly contributed to new optical techniques for functional imaging in vivo over the last decade. Using a conventional epifluorescence microscopy techniques with a scientific-CMOS (sCMOS) camera, we are able to record neural activity from hundreds to thousands of neurons at single-cell resolution. Increasing fraction of observable neurons may provide better insights into neural coding within the larger context of multicellular networks.
